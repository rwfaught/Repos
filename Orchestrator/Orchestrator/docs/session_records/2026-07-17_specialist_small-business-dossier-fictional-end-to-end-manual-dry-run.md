### Fictional business

**Name:** Ozarks Comfort Heating & Air
**Area:** Springfield, Missouri metropolitan area
**Business:** Residential HVAC service, repair, maintenance, and replacement
**Staff:** Owner/general manager, one office coordinator, five technicians, one part-time bookkeeper
**Peak inbound volume:** Approximately 25–40 contacts per weekday

### FS-01 — Owner interview record

**Interviewee:** Daniel Mercer, fictional owner/general manager
**Date:** July 8, 2026
**Duration:** 58 minutes
**Status:** Fictional reported statements, not measured facts

Selected statements:

1. “The biggest problem is that the office gets buried when it gets hot.”
2. “I would guess we lose at least an hour a day cleaning up notes and figuring out what happened.”
3. “Most technicians give us what we need. Maybe one in ten jobs is missing something important.”
4. “I want estimates out the same day whenever possible.”
5. “We usually get standard repair estimates out within 24 hours.”
6. “I do not want a robot answering customers.”
7. “I would consider AI that helps my people write things down better.”
8. “I do not want customer financial data or private household information sent all over the internet.”
9. “The scheduling software probably has an API, but I have never checked.”
10. “I want to start with something we can shut off without disrupting the business.”
11. “I am not spending five figures before I can see it save time.”
12. “The problem might be intake, but the technicians’ notes are where I feel the pain.”

### FS-02 — Office coordinator interview record

**Interviewee:** Melissa Grant, fictional office coordinator
**Date:** July 9, 2026
**Duration:** 64 minutes
**Status:** Fictional reported statements

Selected statements:

1. Peak days produce approximately 30–45 contacts, not 25–40.
2. Around one-third of callers do not know the age or model of their equipment.
3. She enters the same information into scheduling software and often texts a technician separately.
4. “The form on the website misses half the things I need.”

## 5. Current Workflow Reconstruction

### A. Owner-described workflow

1. Customer contacts the office.
2. Coordinator gathers basic details.
3. Coordinator enters the job into scheduling software.
4. Technician receives the job.
5. Technician performs diagnosis and enters notes.
6. Office prepares an estimate where required.
7. Owner reviews significant estimates.
8. Customer receives estimate or service outcome.
9. Office follows up when needed.

Owner’s interpretation:

* Process basically works.
* Peak volume and inconsistent notes create friction.
* Most jobs contain sufficient information.
* Most routine estimates are produced within 24 hours.
* A small AI aid might reduce administrative cleanup.

### B. Staff-described workflow

1. Customer contacts through four distinct channels.
2. Coordinator interprets inconsistent requests.
3. Coordinator often makes a callback.
4. Information is entered into scheduling software.
5. Important context may also be texted separately to a technician.
6. Photographs remain in text threads or on telephones.
7. Technician arrives with variable context.
8. Technician may call the office.
9. Technician completes notes with no enforced structure.
10. Coordinator reconstructs missing facts.
11. Coordinator combines notes, photographs, price sheets, and prior records.
12. Owner reviews many estimates.
13. Work waits when clarification or owner attention is unavailable.
14. Follow-up occurs inconsistently.

### C. Workflow inferred from examples

1. Intake quality varies materially by channel.
2. Safety-sensitive symptoms are not consistently decomposed into structured questions.
3. Website submissions frequently require follow-up.
4. Identity and prior-history matching can fail.
5. Photographs are operationally important but are not reliably attached to job records.
6. Technician notes frequently omit evidence needed for estimates.
7. The estimate process is dependent on informal memory and cross-channel reconstruction.
8. The owner-review queue is a separate source of delay.
9. No stable completion definition exists for “intake complete” or “estimate ready.”
10. Process completion depends on people noticing missing information rather than deterministic completeness checks.

### D. Consolidated current-state map

| Element          | Reconstruction                                                                                                                                |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Trigger          | Customer telephone call, text, email, or website submission                                                                                   |
| Actors           | Customer, coordinator, technician, owner; occasionally bookkeeper                                                                             |
| Primary steps    | Receive request, identify customer, collect symptoms and availability, enter job, hand off, diagnose, document, estimate, review, communicate |
| Handoffs         | Customer→coordinator; coordinator→technician; technician→coordinator; coordinator→owner; owner/coordinator→customer                           |
| Decisions        | Urgency, scheduling, needed information, technician assignment, repair versus estimate, estimate review, follow-up                            |
| Tools            | Scheduling software, email, text messaging, smartphones, paper notes, shared files                                                            |
| Records          | Job record, text thread, email, website form, photographs, technician note, estimate                                                          |
| Delays           | Callbacks, missing equipment data, missing photos, note clarification, price lookup, owner review                                             |
| Rework           | Duplicate entry, repeated customer questions, technician calls, note reconstruction, estimate rewriting                                       |
| Exceptions       | Safety symptoms, vulnerable occupants, missing address, after-hours contact, replacement lead, unresolved prior job                           |
| Completion point | Ambiguous; could mean scheduled, technician dispatched, job closed, estimate sent, or customer followed up                                    |

### E. Unresolved differences

1. The frequency of incomplete jobs is not established.
2. Estimate delay cannot be attributed solely to note quality.
3. Owner-review capacity may be as significant as documentation quality.
4. The owner and coordinator disagree about whether intake or technician notes are the dominant problem.
5. The samples show serious missing fields but do not establish population rates.
6. The company has no agreed operational definition of complete intake.

## 10. Problem and Opportunity Analysis

### Primary problem

**Customer service requests are transformed into technician-ready job context inconsistently, causing avoidable clarification, duplicate entry, and uncertain readiness at dispatch.**

#### Evidence

* Current form lacks equipment, severity, safety, history, access, and communication fields.
* Ten service examples show repeated missing information.
* Coordinator reports duplicate entry and follow-up calls.
* Technician reports missing equipment history and recurring clarification.
* Information is split among job records, text messages, email, photos, and paper.

#### Frequency estimate

Unknown. Reported estimates range from roughly 2–8 materially affected jobs per week, with lesser incompleteness likely more common.

#### Consequences

* Technician begins with incomplete context.
* Office repeats questions.
* Wrong parts or equipment may be carried.
* Safety-sensitive symptoms may be poorly classified.
* Job records do not contain a stable summary.
* Administrative pressure increases during peak demand.

#### Current workaround

Coordinator calls the customer, searches prior records, texts technicians, and relies on memory.

#### Causal assumptions

* More consistent intake will reduce at least some clarification.
* A concise summary is more useful than raw form fields alone.
* Staff will review structured drafts if the process is quick.
* Not all missing context can be obtained before the visit.
* Some burden may result from staffing and software design rather than information quality.

#### Non-AI alternatives

* Redesign the intake form.
* Create mandatory fields and safety questions.
* Use a coordinator checklist.
* Define “technician-ready” completeness.
* Require attachments to the official job record.
* Train staff and audit a sample weekly.

#### No-intervention conditions

No AI component is justified if:

* the redesigned form and checklist solve most of the burden;
* review time exceeds time saved;
* summary errors create more calls or corrections;
* staff do not use the process;
* privacy conditions cannot be satisfied;
* the software already offers an adequate built-in workflow.

### Secondary problem 1

**Technician notes are too inconsistent to support efficient estimate preparation and durable job history.**

Evidence is strong at the sample level but frequency and causal impact are not measured. This should not be the first AI pilot because error consequences are higher, photographs and technical data are fragmented, and estimate delays may be caused by owner-review capacity.

### Secondary problem 2

**Estimate and follow-up work lacks a consistent queue and completion definition.**

The packet indicates delay and inconsistent follow-up, but baseline, ownership, customer outcome, and revenue effect are too weakly established for an AI recommendation. A non-AI queue and status discipline may be the correct first remedy.

## 11. Candidate Intervention Set

* **Problem addressed:** Inconsistent intake and fragmented technician handoff
* **User:** Office coordinator
* **Insertion point:** After structured questions, before job handoff
* **Required input:** Coordinator-entered structured fields and selected customer narrative

* **Burden:** Requires reliable status data and disciplined closeout
* **Privacy implications:** Customer identity, job outcome, estimate status
* **Risks:** Bad prioritization, duplicate work, false queue completeness
* **Dependencies:** Reliable status capture; possibly software export
* **Key uncertainty:** Whether missing follow-up is a data problem or a staffing-capacity problem

## 14. Bounded Pilot Recommendation

### Recommended pilot

`HUMAN_REVIEWED_STRUCTURED_INTAKE_AND_SUMMARY_COMPARISON_PILOT`

### Objective

Determine whether a structured intake process, with an optional human-reviewed AI summary draft, improves technician-ready completeness and reduces clarification burden without increasing intake time, privacy exposure, or staff frustration.

### Participants

* Office coordinator
* One volunteer senior technician
* Owner as reviewer at midpoint and closeout
* Roger as pilot observer and evidence recorder

### Duration and sample

* Ten business days
* Approximately 30 selected non-emergency service requests
* First 10 requests: redesigned structured intake only
* Next 20 requests: same structured intake plus summary draft for coordinator review
* Safety-sensitive requests are excluded from AI summary testing and follow a fixed human-authored escalation checklist

### Permitted data

* Customer name or pilot identifier
* Service address only when operationally required
* Contact details
* Preferred appointment window
* Equipment type, age, brand/model where known
* Customer-described symptoms
* Access constraints
* Recent service history when manually selected
* Coordinator’s structured responses

For a dry technical evaluation, identifiers should be replaced with pilot IDs wherever practical.

### Prohibited data

* Financing or payment data
* Medical details beyond a minimal human safety-escalation flag
* Social Security numbers
* Accounting information
* Unselected email or text history
* Continuous telephone audio
* Employee monitoring data
* Personal-device background access
* Autonomous customer messages
* Automated safety, diagnosis, scheduling, dispatch, or pricing decisions

### Human-review requirement

The coordinator must compare every summary draft against the structured source fields and customer narrative.

The draft cannot enter the official job record until the coordinator:

1. confirms factual consistency;
2. removes unsupported statements;
3. confirms safety flags;
4. marks unresolved missing fields;
5. explicitly approves the summary.

The technician rates the approved summary as useful, partly useful, or not useful after the visit.

### Baseline

* Intake duration
* Percentage of required fields completed
* Number of callbacks before dispatch
* Technician clarification calls
* Technician usefulness rating
* Number and type of coordinator corrections
* Number of safety or material omissions
* Staff burden rating

### Success indicators

1. Required-field completeness improves meaningfully over the old form.
2. Summary drafts require no material factual correction in at least 90% of pilot cases.
3. No safety-critical information is omitted or altered.
4. Median coordinator review time is under two minutes.
5. Technician rates at least 70% of approved summaries as useful.
6. Clarification events decline relative to the baseline sample.
7. Coordinator reports that the process is manageable.
8. No prohibited data are used.
9. The business can stop the process without losing operational records.

These are proposed pilot thresholds, not validated industry standards.

### Failure indicators

* Any uncorrected safety-critical fabrication or omission
* More than 10% of drafts contain material factual errors
* Coordinator review takes as long as writing the summary manually
* Technicians find the summaries distracting or misleading
* Staff bypass mandatory fields
* Customer interaction becomes materially slower or more frustrating
* Personal data are copied outside the approved boundary
* The AI layer adds no measurable benefit beyond the structured form
* A second unsynchronized record becomes necessary

### Rollback

Stop producing summary drafts. Retain the structured intake form and return to the prior handoff procedure. Preserve pilot evidence and approved records; delete transient copies according to the pre-agreed retention rule.

### Estimated effort range

Dry-run estimate:

* Pilot preparation: 6–10 hours
* Form and review checklist: 3–5 hours
* Staff orientation: 1–2 hours
* Daily observation and evidence capture: 30–60 minutes per day
* Midpoint review: 1 hour
* Final analysis and report: 5–8 hours

Total: approximately 20–32 hours of consulting and staff-adjacent effort.

This is not a price recommendation.

### Unresolved prerequisites

1. Define the mandatory technician-ready intake fields.
2. Create a fixed human-authored safety escalation checklist.
3. Establish approved redaction, access, retention, and deletion rules.
4. Determine where pilot records will be stored.
5. Decide whether technical testing will use fictional/redacted cases only.
6. Define a material-error classification.
7. Confirm staff participation.
8. Select no provider or model until these conditions are resolved under a separate boundary.

### Owner decision required

The owner must decide whether to authorize planning for the bounded pilot, including staff participation and a privacy review.

### Explicit non-authorizations

The recommendation does not authorize technical implementation, model testing, external data transfer, software integration, purchase, deployment, customer contact, autonomous operation, or expansion to technician notes.

The pilot is smaller than the uncertainty: it tests one transformation, with one coordinator, one technician, no integration, no customer-facing AI, and an immediate fallback.

Structured fields and the customer’s description would produce a concise technician summary draft. The coordinator would check and approve every draft.

**Benefit:** Potentially reduces rewriting and makes the handoff easier to scan.
**Burden:** Review time and correction.
**Privacy:** Customer information might enter a processing system, so the technical posture must be approved before use.
**Main uncertainty:** Whether the draft saves more time than it consumes.

#### 3. Technician-note assistant

Technician notes, measurements, and selected photographs would produce a structured service-note draft.

**Benefit:** Could improve estimates and job history.
**Burden:** Technician behavior change and technical review.
**Privacy:** May involve audio, photographs, and customer records.
**Main uncertainty:** Accuracy and technician adoption.

#### 4. Follow-up queue assistant

Approved job statuses would produce a prioritized list of estimates and follow-ups.

**Benefit:** Could reduce forgotten work.
**Burden:** Requires accurate statuses and queue maintenance.
**Privacy:** Uses customer and job-status data.
**Main uncertainty:** Whether the real problem is information or staff capacity.

### Recommended first intervention

Run a comparison pilot using:

* a redesigned structured intake form;
* an optional summary draft reviewed by the coordinator;
* no software integration;
* no automatic customer interaction;
* no automated diagnosis, scheduling, messaging, or pricing.

This ranks first because it is:

* directly tied to observed evidence;
* reversible;
* manually testable;
* understandable to staff;
* independent of software integrations;
* able to compare AI against a non-AI improvement;
* capable of producing results within ten business days.

### Expected value and uncertainty

Possible value mechanisms:

* fewer customer callbacks;
* fewer technician clarification calls;
* better preparation;
* less coordinator rewriting;
* clearer job records;
* easier recognition of missing information.

What cannot yet be claimed:

* labor savings;
* revenue increase;
* fewer repeat visits;
* faster estimates;
* customer preference;
* model reliability;
* business-wide return on investment.

A useful result may be that the structured form works and the AI summary does not. That would still be valuable because it prevents unnecessary technology spending.

### Implementation burden

The pilot would require:

* defining mandatory intake fields;
* creating a safety checklist;
* training the coordinator;
* selecting one technician reviewer;
* recording baseline and pilot measurements;
* reviewing every summary;
* defining data handling and deletion;
* analyzing results.

Estimated preparation and evaluation effort is approximately 20–32 combined consulting and staff-adjacent hours. This is a planning estimate, not a quote.

### Privacy and trust considerations

Customer requests may contain:

* names;
* addresses;
* telephone numbers;
* equipment history;
* photographs;
* household access information;
* vulnerable-occupant or safety context.

Before real AI processing, the business must decide:

* what data may be used;
* where processing occurs;
* who has access;
* how long records remain;
* how records are deleted;
* whether customers require notice;
* whether identifiers can be removed;
* how errors are reported.

The pilot should use the minimum information needed. Financing, payment, medical, unrelated email, and personal-device background data are prohibited.

Every output must be reviewed by a person.

### Local-versus-cloud posture

No local or cloud architecture is recommended yet.

A local system might reduce external-data exposure but would create setup, maintenance, security, and capability questions.

A cloud system might be easier to test and could provide stronger capabilities, but customer information may leave the business and become subject to vendor retention, access, and contract terms.

The correct sequence is:

1. prove the workflow manually;
2. define acceptable data handling;
3. establish performance requirements;
4. then compare technical approaches.

Selecting an architecture before that would be choosing a vehicle before deciding whether the road is worth taking.

### Risks and failure modes

* Staff skip fields under pressure.
* Customers become frustrated by too many questions.
* Summary drafts invent or omit facts.
* Staff trust polished wording more than source evidence.
* Safety-sensitive symptoms are mishandled.
* Review time cancels any efficiency.
* Customer information is copied to an unapproved service.
* A second record becomes out of sync with scheduling software.
* Technicians ignore the summary.
* The business mistakes a cleaner document for improved operations.
* The pilot becomes a pretext for broader automation.

### Bounded pilot plan

For ten business days:

1. Use the redesigned intake form for approximately 30 non-emergency requests.
2. Use structured intake only for the first 10.
3. Use structured intake plus a reviewable summary draft for the next 20.
4. Exclude emergency and safety-sensitive requests from AI testing.
5. Require coordinator approval.
6. Ask one technician to rate summary usefulness.
7. Measure completeness, intake time, corrections, callbacks, clarification, and staff burden.
8. Stop immediately if a material privacy or safety problem occurs.

## 19. Capability Gap Re-Ranking

### Rank 1 — Evidence-linked dossier synthesis and transformation

**Description**

A bounded capability that preserves sources, statements, classifications, confidence, contradictions, missing information, constraints, candidate reasoning, and recommendation links while producing an owner-readable dossier.

**Demonstrated burden**

Very high. Approximately 16–20 hours of this dry run involved organizing evidence, extracting statements, preserving conflicts, performing feasibility analysis, and rewriting conclusions without losing qualification.

**Effect on recommendation quality**

Very high. The recommendation depends on seeing exactly which claims are measured, reported, inferred, or missing.

**Uncertainty reduction**

Very high. It would make the causal chain from source to recommendation inspectable.

**Implementation cost or risk**

Medium if introduced as a narrow, human-reviewed transformation over explicit records. High if treated as autonomous analysis.

**Generalization potential**

High. The same transformation applies to research dossiers, claims, investigations, design work, technical reviews, operational assessments, and other supervised workflows.

**Layer classification**

`NEUTRAL_CORE_CANDIDATE`

The neutral core should contain source/fact separation, contradiction visibility, missing-information tracking, recommendation linkage, and human disposition. Domain-specific HVAC scoring and consulting language should remain outside it.

### Rank 2 — Guided intake sufficiency and source-packet assembly

**Description**

A structured process for defining objective, scope, source permissions, workflow, participants, constraints, evidence sufficiency, missing material, and stop conditions.

**Demonstrated burden**

Moderate to high. The manual packet was usable, but its quality determined every downstream conclusion.

**Effect on recommendation quality**

High. Weak intake creates artificial certainty or endless clarification.

**Uncertainty reduction**

High at the beginning of the engagement.

**Implementation cost or risk**

Low to medium for templates and structured records; higher for adaptive automated interviewing.

**Generalization potential**

High.

**Layer classification**

`NEUTRAL_CORE_CANDIDATE_WITH_USE_CASE_SPECIFIC_INTAKE_TEMPLATES`

### Rank 3 — Engagement outcome and disposition evidence capture

**Description**

A record of effort, revisions, comprehension, trust, privacy concerns, value perception, decision latency, client disposition, implementation willingness, follow-up demand, and willingness to pay.

**Demonstrated burden**

Low to moderate.

**Effect on recommendation quality**

Moderate. It does not create the recommendation, but it reveals whether the workflow serves its strategic purpose.

**Uncertainty reduction**

High for product and consulting decisions.

**Implementation cost or risk**

Low.

**Generalization potential**

Medium. Human disposition generalizes strongly; willingness-to-pay and consulting evidence are use-case-specific.

`MIXED_NEUTRAL_CORE_AND_CONSULTING_LAYER`

### Re-ranking conclusion

Evidence-linked synthesis remains first, but the dry run sharpens the target.

The missing capability is not generic “provenance” or a broad Dossier Workspace. It is a narrower transformation seam:

> Turn an explicitly bounded source packet and classified statement register into a reviewable recommendation package while preserving every material contradiction, gap, qualification, and decision gate.

That is the narrowest capability whose absence visibly inflated both effort and error risk.
